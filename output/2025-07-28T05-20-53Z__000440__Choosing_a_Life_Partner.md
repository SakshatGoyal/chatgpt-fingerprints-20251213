### The Theory of Opposites: Half-True, and Therefore Dangerous

To seek your opposite can be *useful*—if, and only if, your aim is compensation, not companionship. The shy man who marries the extrovert may gain access to networks and social insulation he lacks. But what he gains in breadth, he may lose in depth. The essential tension here is this:

> **The same trait that balances you at first may, over time, alienate you.**

Opposites excite, but similarity endures. You may be fascinated by what you lack, but when conflict arises—as it always does—it is shared values, not complementary traits, that prevent ruin. I do not mean shared hobbies or similar music tastes; I mean agreement on how power should be exercised in the household, what loyalties take precedence, and how one confronts adversity.

### The Proper Calculation

A life partner is not a mirror, nor a counterweight—they are a *co-ruler*. Think of it politically: would you appoint a minister whose instincts always diverge from your own? Perhaps to challenge you, yes—but only if he remains loyal. The same applies in marriage.

> **Choose not your opposite, but your strategic complement.**

This means someone whose *strengths reinforce your ambition*, whose temperament tempers your flaws without resenting them, and whose loyalty is not shaken by time or temptation. You do not need their soul to be unlike yours—you need their mission to align with yours.

### Traits to Seek, Regardless of Type

1. **Emotional discipline** – passion fades, but composure preserves the union.
2. **Shared long-term vision** – two oxen must pull the same plow.
3. **Mutual admiration** – not love alone, but respect fortified by tested character.
4. **Low volatility** – opposites often bring chaos; avoid someone addicted to drama.

### Final Counsel

In your youth, opposites may seduce you. They are novel, stimulating, exotic. But novelty is a fleeting mistress. In the long march of time, it is similarity of principle—not personality—that builds empires. Choose a partner not for balance, but for cohesion.
