# **What “training to failure” really means**
When you do a movement—say push-ups—your muscles are working with a mixture of muscle fibers:

- **Slow-twitch fibers** (endurance, easy to activate)  
- **Fast-twitch fibers** (big, strong, responsible for visible muscle growth)

Your body likes to save energy, so it uses the **smallest, weakest fibers first**.  
You only start using the big, growth-oriented fibers **when the small ones are tired**.

Training *close* to failure is the point where:

- the easy fibers are done  
- the harder, growth-capable fibers are finally recruited  

This is why the last 3–5 reps of a set matter more than the first 20.

---

# **Why train to failure?**
Because it ensures **maximum muscle fiber recruitment**.

When you push until you literally cannot do another rep with good form:

- Muscle fibers break down  
- The body repairs them thicker and stronger  
- You activate the fibers responsible for size  

Think of it like digging deep into the soil—growth happens when you tap the deeper layers.

---

Not always.

Studies show:

- You don’t NEED to go to **absolute** failure  
- But you need to go **near** failure (0–3 reps left in the tank), especially for bodyweight exercises

Absolute failure = when you physically cannot complete another rep even if your life depended on it.

Near failure = muscles are burning, form is slowing, maybe you could squeeze 1 more but not cleanly.

Both work.  
Absolute failure is more tiring but not more effective long-term.
