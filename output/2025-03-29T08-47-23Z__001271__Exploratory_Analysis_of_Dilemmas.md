### DILEMMA TYPES

| **Dilemma Type** | **Definition** | **What to Look For** | **Tagging Cautions** | **Good Example** | **Misuse Example** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Ambiguity** | Decision lacks clarity due to missing/conflicting data or uncertain goals. | Disagreement on problem, multiple plausible options, leaders stalling for clarity. | Don’t tag unless ambiguity made the decision hard—uncertainty alone isn’t enough. | “We didn’t know how to define success for the pilot, so each team ran it differently.” | “We didn’t hit the KPIs.” |
| **Control** | Tension about who owns the decision or how authority is distributed. | Power deferrals, re-escalations, centralized overrides, unclear governance. | Must involve power tension—not just ownership confusion. | “We formed a cross-functional team, but no one knew who had final say.” | “The project had no clear owner.” |
| **Visibility** | High visibility makes execs optimize for optics instead of truth. | Fear of public failure, reputational risk, decisions driven by perception. | Don’t confuse with communication after the fact—this is about visibility affecting the *decision*. | “We didn’t want to change direction publicly, so we stuck with the plan longer than we should have.” | “We announced the strategy at all-hands.” |
| **Pace** | Conflict between urgency and getting it right. | Speed pressure, shortcuts, tension between moving fast and doing it thoroughly. | Tag only if speed is a central factor in the difficulty—not just mentioned. | “We knew the old process took too long, but skipping it introduced risk.” | “The project took six months.” |
| **Cultural Fit** | The strategy clashes with organizational norms or identity. | Legacy behaviors, emotional resistance, unspoken discomfort with change. | Don’t tag unless culture *blocked* execution—not just a mention of culture. | “We pushed a test-and-learn model, but teams felt that failing publicly wasn’t acceptable.” | “This might require culture change.” |
| **Trade-off Blindness** | Leaders refuse to name or resolve conflicting priorities. | Multiple goals treated as non-negotiable, overcommitment, no de-prioritization. | Don’t tag if trade-offs were made—only when they were *avoided*. | “Leadership said cost and quality were both non-negotiable, so teams froze.” | “We picked Option A over B.” |
| **Capability Doubt** | Execs hesitate because they don’t believe the org can execute. | Fear of team readiness, skill gaps, lack of confidence in delivery ability. | Don’t confuse with actual lack of capacity (that’s a failure mode). | “We had the strategy, but weren’t sure if our teams could pull it off in time.” | “We didn’t have enough people.” |

### FAILURE MODES

| **Failure Mode** | **Definition** | **What to Look For** | **Tagging Cautions** | **Good Example** | **Misuse Example** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Signal Denial** | Early warnings or feedback were ignored or downplayed. | Customer feedback dismissed, internal data suppressed, action delayed despite signals. | Signal must have been visible and known—not just “no one saw it coming.” | “Customer service flagged the issue early, but it was brushed off as noise.” | “We didn’t see the disruption coming.” |
| **Overconfidence** | Leaders misjudged the difficulty, speed, or buy-in required. | Unrealistic timelines, surprise at execution complexity, leadership optimism. | Must reflect *misjudgment*—not just failure. | “We expected to shift behavior in 6 weeks, but it took 6 months.” | “The team didn’t hit their goals.” |
| **Structural Misfit** | Processes, systems, or governance didn’t support the strategy. | Workarounds, rigid process blocks, tech-tool mismatches. | Must show a *mismatch*—not just “we used a clunky tool.” | “We had the new workflow, but it couldn’t handle edge cases—so people worked around it.” | “The tool was clunky.” |
| **Translation Breakdown** | Strategic intent was misunderstood or diluted during execution. | Confused execution teams, multiple interpretations, vague goals. | Tag only if strategy was *misunderstood*—not intentionally ignored. | “Strategy said ‘empowerment,’ but no one defined what that meant for our team.” | “Teams didn’t follow the strategy.” |
| **Cultural Friction** | Organizational values or norms resisted the change. | Behavioral resistance despite verbal agreement, subtle rejection of “new ways.” | Requires evidence of values clashing—not generic resistance. | “Experimentation was framed as risky—even though we said we valued learning.” | “There was resistance.” |
| **Capacity Collapse** | Teams lacked time, bandwidth, or tools to execute effectively. | Teams overwhelmed, over-committed, lacking key enablers. | Don’t confuse with *doubt about capability*—this is about actual breakdown. | “Teams couldn’t take on the new process while keeping BAU going.” | “The strategy failed.” |
| **Symbolic Compliance** | Stakeholders performed agreement but resisted or ignored in practice. | Passive sabotage, ritualized buy-in, private rejection of strategy. | Must show contradiction between support and behavior—not just disagreement. | “Strategy was endorsed at the town hall—but execution teams quietly kept using the old system.” | “There was disagreement.” |
