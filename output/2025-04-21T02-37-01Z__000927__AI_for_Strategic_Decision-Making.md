### 1. **Start with the Three-Question Product Lens**

Whenever we’re defining product behavior, it’s helpful to zoom out first:

1. **What real people-problem are we solving?**  
   Are executives struggling with strategic clarity? Are they lacking diverse perspectives? Are their decisions moving too slowly or being made on gut feel alone?

   → Prompt: _Can we map the most common “job to be done” moments — e.g. “I need to align my team on a GTM shift” or “I’m preparing for a board discussion on competitive risk”?_ These are the friction points the AI should be designed to meet.

2. **How do we know it’s a real problem?**  
   What are the signals that leaders are feeling these constraints today? Is it in the post-mortems, the reorg memos, the constant strategy revisions?

   → Prompt: _Do we have real-world stories (even anonymized) that show what “bad” and “good” strategy-making looks like in each cluster?_

3. **Why now?**  
   The AI space is noisy. To earn a seat in an executive’s toolkit, the timing has to feel right.

   → Prompt: _How does AI change what’s possible for strategic work that used to require whiteboards, team offsites, or consultants?_

---

### 2. **Anchor AI Behaviors in the “Purpose-People-Process” Filter**

Each AI interaction should map to at least one of these pillars:

- **Purpose**: Can the AI clarify the "why" behind a strategy? E.g., reframe a vague growth goal into a clearer theory of value creation.

- **People**: Can the AI surface unspoken assumptions about stakeholders? E.g., prompt the leader to consider how a competitor, customer, or internal team might respond.

- **Process**: Can the AI structure a messy thought process? E.g., guide through a positioning matrix or run a “pre-mortem” exercise on a roadmap plan.

→ Try mapping each of your four strategic clusters to this triad. Where is the current process weakest? Where do execs need the most scaffolding?

---

### 3. **Define Product Behaviors as Strategic “Maneuvers”**

Rather than generic AI capabilities (e.g. “it asks questions”), define behaviors as *strategy maneuvers*, like:

- **“Reframe the risk”**: When a leader is over-indexing on one competitive threat, the AI introduces an adjacent risk or analogy from another industry.
- **“Contrast the assumptions”**: The AI helps compare two paths by surfacing underlying assumptions and challenging them.
- **“Map the stakeholder chessboard”**: The AI walks through likely stakeholder reactions to a proposed move.

→ Prompt: _What would a strong playbook of these strategic maneuvers look like, tailored to each pattern cluster?_

---

### 4. **Build a Feedback Flywheel, Even Without Proprietary Data**

Even if you don’t access company-specific data, you *can* build contextual sensitivity by:

- Allowing executives to “narrate” their situation — capturing high-signal language about tensions, fears, or hopes.
- Tracking which prompts, questions, or frameworks yield the most reflection or revision.
- Offering summaries or “thinking artifacts” that leaders can refine or share (e.g., “Here’s a 1-pager you can use in your next SLT meeting”).

→ Prompt: _How might we design lightweight artifacts or reflection outputs that let the AI learn what’s working — without storing sensitive data?_
