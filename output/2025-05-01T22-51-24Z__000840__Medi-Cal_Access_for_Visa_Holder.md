Overall, this is a viable backup that should be pursued *only if* Medi-Cal or presumptive Medi-Cal is facing delays.

- **Concerns:** These county programs usually **restrict care to within that county’s providers**. For instance, Healthy SF only covers services at San Francisco clinics/hospitals and their pharmacy network. If she is staying in a different county (say, Santa Clara), she couldn’t use Healthy SF unless she *moved to SF or used a friend’s address*. It’s important to work with the program in **her actual county of residence** for continuity. Another concern is that coverage might not be as comprehensive or long-term as Medi-Cal. They often cover primary care and generics, but some expensive brand-name psychiatric drugs might require special assistance even within those programs. Also, once Medi-Cal approves, she would transition out of the county program. Legally, there is no issue with using these services; they are locally funded and **do not affect immigration status**. They exist precisely because counties want to keep their residents healthy and out of ERs. Administratively, if she started in a county program, she must keep track of transferring any care or prescriptions to Medi-Cal later. But county case workers can help with that. 

- **References & Contacts:** Each county has an enrollment office or information line:
  - **San Francisco – Healthy SF:** Call 415-615-4555 or visit an enrollment site (like SF General Hospital). They explicitly accept all SF residents regardless of immigration, for comprehensive care.
  - **Alameda – HealthPAC:** Enrollment info is on Alameda Health Care Services Agency’s site【12†L1-L8】【12†L15-L18】; one can apply at Alameda Health System clinics.

  - **Contra Costa – CARES:** This is run in partnership with community clinics (like La Clínica, etc.), reachable via the Contra Costa Health Services helpline.
  
  Additionally, organizations like **Community Health Centers** can guide enrollment. ## Pathway 5: County Behavioral Health Services for Serious Mental Illness

**Description:** California’s county mental health departments provide services to residents with **serious mental illness (SMI)** – often regardless of ability to pay – using state and local funds (e.g., the Mental Health Services Act). Schizophrenia is typically considered a serious mental illness, which means the county **Behavioral Health** or **Mental Health** department has a mandate to ensure treatment is available. If she cannot access Medi-Cal immediately, she may receive **psychiatric care and medications through county mental health programs**.

- **Eligibility Rationale:** Each county’s mental health plan serves Medi-Cal beneficiaries *and* indigent residents with serious mental illness. Since she is currently under a private psychiatrist’s care, she hasn’t tapped county services yet. But she is eligible to do so as a resident with a diagnosed schizophrenia condition. For example, if she were in Santa Clara County, the **Behavioral Health Services Department** could intake her (via the Mental Health Call Center or a clinic) because schizophrenia is a qualifying condition for county support. There is usually no income test – the criteria is the severity of mental health diagnosis and residency in the county. Immigration status is **not a barrier**; counties do not turn away SMI patients due to being non-citizens. In fact, part of the justification for Medi-Cal expansion was that counties were already spending local funds on these individuals; but while she awaits Medi-Cal, the county can step in.

- **Required Steps/Documentation:** She or her family can **contact the county mental health access line** (every county has a 24/7 access/crisis line for mental health). For example, Alameda County’s ACCESS line (1-800-491-9099) or Santa Clara’s Mental Health Services Call Center, etc., and explain that a 62-year-old with schizophrenia needs medication and cannot afford it. Typically, they will schedule an assessment or direct her to a **county mental health clinic**. She may need to undergo an intake evaluation by a county mental health clinician to officially enroll in county services. If possible, having a **referral from her current psychiatrist** or a copy of her diagnosis/treatment plan would be helpful to smooth the process. Documentation of residency (an address) is useful but often not rigidly required for initial crisis services. The county might ask her to sign up for Medi-Cal concurrently (and they might even help with that), but they can provide services in the interim. Key step: get assigned to a county psychiatrist or case manager who can prescribe her medication and provide samples or fill prescriptions through the county pharmacy system. Many county mental health clinics have access to **Patient Assistance Programs** for psychiatric medications and can provide meds at low or no cost while a patient is enrolled in their care.

- **Timeline to Access:** **Short-term (days to a week).** If she communicates that she is at risk of running out of medication (which could lead to decompensation), the mental health department may treat this with urgency. Some counties have **same-day or next-day intake** for urgent medication needs. In crisis, mobile crisis teams or crisis clinics can also supply short-term meds. Within a few days, she could have an appointment at a county outpatient clinic, and they could potentially provide a prescription or samples immediately. For instance, a county psychiatrist could give a **starter supply** of an antipsychotic or enroll her in the county pharmacy program (counties often have arrangements to cover medications for their indigent clients). This pathway can thus bridge a gap of a week or two until Medi-Cal kicks in. Even after Medi-Cal begins, she might continue with county mental health if her needs are intensive (Medi-Cal’s mental health benefit for schizophrenia is actually delivered by the county mental health plan anyway in California’s system).

- **Feasibility (1–5):** **4 (High).** The county mental health system exists to serve exactly this population – people with severe mental illness who might be uninsured or unable to afford care. The main challenges are bureaucratic (getting an intake scheduled) and personal (transitioning care to a new doctor). Some individuals encounter wait times for non-crisis appointments, but given her diagnosis, they will likely prioritize her. Feasibility is high because it does not depend on complex eligibility – a phone call and showing up for an appointment is usually enough to start receiving help. The reason it’s not a full 5 is that it might require switching providers (which she might be hesitant to do, since she trusts her current psychiatrist), and navigating a new system can be stressful. However, she can still stay in touch with her private psychiatrist while using county services for medication assistance as a stop-gap.

- **Concerns:** **Continuity of care** is a consideration – moving to a county clinic means a different doctor and possibly a different approach to treatment. If she has a good rapport with her current psychiatrist, she should inform him/her that she may need to use county resources temporarily. Sometimes private psychiatrists and county providers can coordinate (with patient consent) to ensure a smooth handoff or shared care. **Medication formulary**: County programs might prefer certain medications (often they have access to generics and certain brands via their pharmacy). If she’s on a very new or specialized (expensive) drug, the county may try to switch her to an equivalent that they can obtain, or they might use patient assistance programs to get that drug. This needs to be discussed in the intake – she should bring a list of her current medications. **Administrative**: There may be paperwork to officially enroll her as a client of the county mental health plan. She might also be asked to sign applications for benefits (they often help clients apply for Medi-Cal or SSI if needed). None of these should deter her; it’s standard procedure. **Legal**: No negative legal implications; receiving mental health services as an uninsured immigrant is protected and confidential. In fact, there are **legal advocacy groups** (like the Law Foundation of Silicon Valley’s Mental Health Advocacy Project) that ensure people with mental illness get access to treatment regardless of status【21†L438-L447】【21†L449-L458】. If she faces any pushback, she can seek legal aid (see *Support Resources*).

- **References & Examples:** It’s well documented that county mental health plans serve as providers for Medi-Cal **and the uninsured SMI population**【21†L444-L452】. For instance, the Santa Clara County Behavioral Health Services website and NAMI local chapters encourage those without insurance to reach out for county services, highlighting that cost should not be a barrier. The **Law Foundation of Silicon Valley – Health/Mental Health Program** specifically assists individuals with mental health disabilities in accessing benefits and county services【21†L438-L446】. A recommended contact is the county’s mental health access number (which varies by county, easily found on county websites or via 211). Engaging with these services can ensure she has **continuous access to her medication** and support, especially during the gap before Medi-Cal coverage is active.

## Pathway 6: Pharmaceutical Company Patient Assistance Programs (PAP) for Medications

**Description:** Most pharmaceutical manufacturers offer **Patient Assistance Programs** that provide free or discounted medications to low-income patients who are **uninsured**. Since schizophrenia often involves expensive antipsychotic drugs, these PAPs can be a crucial resource. They typically supply brand-name medications free of charge for a set period (often in 90-day shipments) to eligible individuals through their doctor.

- **Eligibility Rationale:** Patient Assistance Programs are generally intended for people who **lack insurance coverage for the medication** and have low income. Our patient has no insurance and no income, so she is an excellent candidate financially. Each medication’s program has specific criteria. Common requirements are: U.S. residency (she qualifies as she is residing in California, even if not a permanent resident), a valid prescription for the drug, and income usually below some multiple of the federal poverty level (she is at $0 income, well below thresholds). **Immigration status is not asked** – the concern is lack of insurance and inability to afford the drug. For example, if her schizophrenia is managed with **Risperidone (Risperdal)** or **Paliperidone (Invega)** by Janssen, that company’s PAP could provide it free for 1 year with a doctor’s application. Similarly, **Abilify (aripiprazole)** has a program through Otsuka, **Latuda (lurasidone)** through Sunovion, **Zyprexa or Olanzapine** (Lilly Cares program if brand needed), etc. If her medication is a generic (like generic risperidone), PAPs may not cover it (they focus on brands), but generics are usually much cheaper out-of-pocket. Should she be on a newer long-acting injectable or a costly brand-only drug, PAP is a strong option.

- **Required Documentation/Steps:** Each pharmaceutical PAP requires an **application**, usually a form to be filled out by **both the patient and the prescribing doctor**. Typically:
  - The patient fills out personal information, income (can state $0 and explain she is supported by family), and signs a declaration.
  - The doctor provides medical information (diagnosis, prescription details) and signs, often including their license number and shipping address for the medication.
  - Proof of income may be required – in her case, with $0 income, a **“Letter of Support”** or a form attesting that she has no income might be needed. Alternatively, some programs accept a statement like “no income, living with relative who provides support” on the form.
  - Proof of residency might be requested (some programs want to ensure the patient is in the U.S.). A copy of a utility bill or any official mail at her current address could suffice. Her **passport ID** will serve as identification.
  - The application is then submitted (via fax or online portal or mail) by the doctor’s office or the patient. Many programs now have online enrollment that the doctor’s office can do quickly.
  
She should talk to her psychiatrist about this option – **often doctors are familiar with PAPs** for expensive psychiatric meds and have the forms ready. Given the urgency, ask the doctor’s office to assist in completing and faxing the forms **as soon as possible**. Once approved, the medication is usually shipped **to the doctor’s office** or to her address in a few days to a couple of weeks. 

- **Timeline to Access:** **Moderate (1–3 weeks)** – PAP approval and delivery can vary. Some programs approve within days if done electronically, and they might ship overnight; others take a week or two. This won’t solve a “need medication tomorrow” scenario, but it can kick in within a couple of weeks, providing free medication for the next several months. As a stop-gap, many programs will also accept an application if she’s *about to have Medi-Cal* but not yet – however, once she gets Medi-Cal, she typically becomes ineligible for PAP (since she’ll have insurance coverage). Therefore, PAP is most useful either if Medi-Cal enrollment fails or to cover a short gap before Medi-Cal. Notably, **some manufacturers offer “bridge” or “quick start” programs** that can provide an immediate one-time supply while the main application is processed. For example, if she’s on an injectable and due for a dose, the rep or program might arrange a sample or one-month supply quickly. Her psychiatrist should inquire with the drug representative about any **emergency medication supply programs**.

- **Feasibility (1–5):** **4 (High).** The PAP route is highly feasible given her lack of income and insurance – she essentially meets the core criteria for all such programs. The only reason it’s not a 5 is timing and paperwork: it requires cooperation from her doctor and a short wait for processing. But manufacturers generally want to assist patients in need (especially for chronic conditions like schizophrenia) because it’s part of their patient care programs. If her doctor is willing to spend a little time on the application, she could secure thousands of dollars worth of medication for free. Feasibility also depends on which drug: nearly all antipsychotic manufacturers have PAPs. (If by chance she’s on a very old generic that costs only $20 at retail, PAP isn’t necessary; but expensive ones – easily $500+ a month – are where PAP shines.)

- **Concerns:** **Short-term solution:** PAPs typically approve free medication for a fixed duration (e.g. one year, after which you must reapply). If she’s planning to get Medi-Cal, she will likely stop using PAP once Medi-Cal covers her meds (using both is not allowed – one cannot have Medi-Cal pay for a drug and also get free drug from the manufacturer). She should be careful not to “double-dip.” Legally, no issues – receiving free medication from a private program has no public charge or immigration effect, and it’s not public aid. Privacy is respected; the pharmaceutical company only uses her info to determine eligibility and ship the drug. One caution: if she *does* get Medi-Cal activated while a PAP supply is ongoing, she or her doctor should notify the PAP to avoid conflicts (since after that she wouldn’t technically qualify as uninsured). Another concern is **continuity**: if she starts on PAP medication supply, she must ensure that when it runs out, she has Medi-Cal or another plan to continue, so there’s no lapse. PAPs also usually require a U.S. address – she has that (relative’s home). A minor administrative concern is that some PAPs may list that you shouldn’t have other insurance – since she’s in the process of Medi-Cal, she is currently uninsured, which is true. She should answer honestly on applications (e.g., “Do you have insurance?” → “No”, since Medi-Cal is not active yet).

- **References & Resources:** A number of resources aggregate information on PAPs:
  - The **Partnership for Prescription Assistance (PPARx)** provides a search tool for assistance programs for specific drugs【2†L233-L242】【21†L424-L432】.
  - **NeedyMeds** is a nonprofit with lists of medication assistance programs and even downloadable forms【2†L233-L242】.
  - **RxAssist** and **RxHope** are similar resources listing PAP details【2†L233-L242】.
  
For example, NAMI (National Alliance on Mental Illness) highlights that PAPs can help patients get their needed psychiatric drugs for free or low cost【2†L219-L228】【2†L233-L242】. She can look up her specific medication on those websites or ask her doctor to do so. As a case in point, if she is on **Latuda (lurasidone)**, the manufacturer’s site has a patient assistance section where the form can be downloaded and filled out – typically requiring a doctor’s signature and a proof of income which in her case is a zero-income statement. Many applications can be faxed for quick processing. **Recommended action**: Make a list of her medications (name and dosage) and use a resource like NeedyMeds or the drug maker’s website to find the PAP forms. With her psychiatrist’s help, send them in ASAP. This can run in parallel with her Medi-Cal application, ensuring she has a fallback for medication in the next few weeks.

## Pathway 7: Prescription Discount Programs, Samples, and Interim Solutions

**Description:** To address the **immediate need (next few days)**, there are a few practical options for obtaining medication at a reduced cost or free, without formal enrollment in programs:
- **Medication Samples from Doctor:** Psychiatrists often have sample packs provided by pharmaceutical reps, especially for newer medications. A candid request to her doctor for **samples to cover the next couple of weeks** could yield enough pills or injections to tide her over【2†L209-L218】.
- **Pharmacy Discount Cards/Coupons:** Programs like **GoodRx** or the **California Rx Card** offer significant discounts on prescription prices【2†L233-L242】. GoodRx is a free website/app where she can print a coupon for her medication and present it at a pharmacy to get a lower cash price. This could bring down, for example, a $300 medication to perhaps $30 or $50 in some cases. The California Rx Card is a free statewide discount card available to all residents【15†L41-L44】.
- **Generic Medications:** If her current prescription is a brand-name drug with no generic, ask the doctor if a **therapeutically similar generic** could be used temporarily. Many first-generation or older antipsychotics are very inexpensive. For instance, a month’s supply of generic **haloperidol or risperidone** might cost under $20 with a Walmart or Costco $4 generics plan. While the efficacy and side effect profiles differ, a short-term use of a cheaper alternative could be considered to avoid a lapse in treatment.
- **Short Fills:** Pharmacists can sometimes dispense a partial quantity of medication if you can’t pay for the full month. She might be able to buy just one week’s worth of pills with the cash she or her family can spare, and then replenish once insurance/PAP kicks in.
- **Charitable or Low-Cost Pharmacies:** In some areas, non-profit pharmacies or programs (like *St. Vincent de Paul charitable pharmacy* or community clinic dispensaries) provide medications at low cost or free. The Bay Area’s major safety net clinics (e.g., Marin Community Clinics, Tri-City Health Center, etc.) have pharmacies that offer drugs on a **sliding scale**. If she accesses a community clinic doctor through the county programs, they can often dispense samples or use **340B discount pricing** to give her meds at lower cost.

- **Eligibility Rationale:** These options largely don’t have strict eligibility criteria – they are available to anyone. GoodRx, for example, can be used by anyone regardless of income or status (it’s just a coupon system). Doctor’s samples are at the physician’s discretion to give to patients who need them. The only “eligibility” for charitable sources would be being uninsured and low-income, which she is – for instance, some charity pharmacies might require a referral or simple intake to confirm need.

- **Required Steps:** 
  - **Ask the Psychiatrist:** She should explain her situation (if she hasn’t already) – that she is applying for Medi-Cal, but in the meantime cannot afford the next refill. Most psychiatrists will respond by either providing samples (if available) or possibly prescribing a cheaper medication for interim use【2†L209-L218】. They might also write a letter to a pharmacy or advocate on her behalf to get a short supply.
  - **Use GoodRx or Discounts:** She (or her family) can go online to GoodRx.com, search her medication and dosage, and find the lowest price at pharmacies nearby. GoodRx will provide a coupon code or card. When she goes to the pharmacy, she can show the GoodRx coupon (either printed or on a smartphone) and pay the discounted price. It’s important to note she cannot combine this with insurance; it’s an out-of-pocket option, but at a reduced rate. **Needymeds Drug Discount Card** is another similar tool recommended by NAMI【2†L233-L242】 – it’s free and can be printed from the Needymeds website and used at many pharmacies.
  - **Community Clinics/Pharmacies:** If she can identify a Federally Qualified Health Center (FQHC) or free clinic near her, she might visit them and explain her urgent need. Some clinics have **sample closets** or can enroll patients in pharmacy assistance on the spot. For instance, Berkeley Free Clinic or Clinic by the Bay (SF) might not specifically stock antipsychotics for walk-ins, but if they have a volunteer psychiatrist, they might help. It could be hit-or-miss, but worth exploring if other avenues are delayed.
  - **Family Help:** As a last resort, if the cost after GoodRx is still high, her relatives might consider helping pay for a small supply. For example, if GoodRx brings a $800/month drug down to $100 for a month, perhaps they can manage $100 for one month to keep her stable until Medi-Cal coverage starts. Maintaining treatment continuity for schizophrenia is critical, so even paying out-of-pocket for one month is an investment in preventing a crisis or hospitalization (which would be far more costly and traumatic).

- **Timeline to Access:** **Immediate (1–3 days).** These strategies can be employed right away:
  - Doctor’s samples: same day at her next appointment or by contacting the office (the psychiatrist might allow pickup of samples without a full visit if they understand the urgency).
  - GoodRx coupon: same day – she just needs to go to a pharmacy with the coupon. She should call the pharmacy first to ensure they have the medication in stock and ask them to run the GoodRx price quote for her prescription.
  - Generic switch: could be done with a phone call to the doctor to send a new prescription to the pharmacy, which could then be filled the next day.
  - These are bridging options intended to cover the **next few days to 2 weeks** until the larger solutions (Medi-Cal or PAP) take effect.

- **Feasibility (1–5):** **5 (High)** for at least one of these measures. Getting some medication immediately is very feasible:
  - GoodRx and similar discounts are straightforward and widely used; pharmacies honor them routinely.
  - Most doctors in mental health are sympathetic to cost issues and will offer samples or adjust treatment if necessary【2†L209-L218】.
  - Buying a limited supply is technically feasible if funds can be mustered.
  
There might be some trial-and-error (for instance, not all doctors have samples of every drug, and GoodRx discounts vary by pharmacy), but given the menu of options, she should be able to walk away with at least a partial medication supply within a day or two. This pathway is essentially about **ensuring no gap in medication** during the transition period.

- **Concerns:** These measures are **temporary fixes** and not sustainable long-term. They assume that within a few weeks, a more permanent solution (like Medi-Cal or PAP) will be in place. Another concern is **consistency of medication** – switching to a different drug or different supply source could confuse the treatment plan. Any change in medication (like using an alternative generic) should be done with medical guidance to avoid destabilization. If using GoodRx or paying cash, one should try to stay on the same medication and dose if possible. Using samples is great but often you might get varying quantities or brands; she needs to keep track and ensure she doesn’t run out unexpectedly. Also, some pharmacies might look at a GoodRx price for a high-cost drug and only dispense a partial because of inventory – clear communication with the pharmacist is key. **No legal issues** here except that using certain discount cards requires one to not use insurance for that transaction (which is fine since she currently has none). GoodRx usage does not affect any future insurance; it’s purely a pricing mechanism. 

- **References:** NAMI’s guidance for those without insurance includes **“talk with your doctor”** about samples or cheaper alternatives, and using **prescription discount cards** like GoodRx【2†L209-L218】【2†L233-L242】. The **California Rx Card** is mentioned as a free statewide prescription assistance program open to all residents【15†L41-L44】 (it functions similarly to GoodRx). Many patients have bridged short gaps by paying out-of-pocket with discounts and later getting reimbursed once insurance kicks in – *note:* Medi-Cal, if retroactively effective, might even reimburse some of those out-of-pocket medication costs if they occurred in a month she was later deemed eligible, but that process can be complex. She can keep receipts just in case. Overall, these interim steps are well-known and advisable to ensure she **does not interrupt her schizophrenia treatment**, which is paramount for her health and safety.
